Career

Downey began building upon his theater roles, making his debut on stage at the Geva Theatre Center in Alms for the Middle Class for a three-week run. He also performed in the short-lived off-Broadway musical American Passion at the Joyce Theater, produced by Norman Lear. In 1984, Downey got a part in the film Firstborn, where he met Sarah Jessica Parker, and the pair started dating. In 1985, he was part of the new, younger cast hired for Saturday Night Live. Downey has said that Anthony Michael Hall, whom he met and became friends with on the set of Weird Science, helped him get the audition. However, he and most of the new crew were dropped and replaced following a year of poor ratings and criticism of the new cast's comedic talents. Rolling Stone magazine even named Downey the worst SNL cast member in its entire run, stating that the "Downey Fail sums up everything that makes SNL great." That same year, Downey had a dramatic acting breakthrough when he played James Spader's sidekick in Tuff Turf and then a bully in John Hughes's Weird Science. Molly Ringwald wanted him for the role of Duckie in Hughes's film Pretty in Pink, but the part went to Jon Cryer. He went on to star with Ringwald in his first lead role in The Pick-up Artist. Because of these and other coming-of-age films Downey did during the 1980s, he is sometimes named as a member of the Brat Pack. Downey moved to Hollywood and lived with fellow actors Billy Zane—who later appeared with him in Only You—Sarah Jessica Parker, and Kiefer Sutherland, who starred with Downey in 1969. In 1987, Downey played Julian Wells, a drug-addicted rich boy whose life rapidly spirals out of control, in the film version of Bret Easton Ellis's novel Less than Zero. His performance, described by Janet Maslin in The New York Times as "desperately moving," was widely praised, though Downey has said that for him "the role was like the ghost of Christmas Future" since his drug habit resulted in his becoming an "exaggeration of the character" in real life. Soon after completing the film, Downey went into rehab for the first time; this episode would be followed by a number of interventions and stints in rehab over the next decade prior to his arrest. Less than Zero drove Downey into films with bigger budgets and names, such as Chances Are with Cybill Shepherd and Ryan O'Neal, Air America with Mel Gibson, and Soapdish with Sally Field, Kevin Kline, Cathy Moriarty, and Whoopi Goldberg. In 1992, he starred as Charlie Chaplin in Chaplin, a role for which he prepared extensively—learning how to play the violin and even training to play tennis left-handed. He worked with a personal coach to imitate Chaplin's posture and way of carrying himself. The role garnered Downey an Academy Award nomination for Best Actor at the Academy Awards ceremony, though he ultimately lost to Al Pacino in Scent of a Woman. In 1993, he appeared in the films Heart and Souls with Alfre Woodard and Kyra Sedgwick and Short Cuts with Matthew Modine and Julianne Moore, along with writing a documentary about the 1992 presidential campaigns titled The Last Party. He later starred in the 1994 films Only You with Marisa Tomei and Natural Born Killers with Woody Harrelson. Subsequent credits include Restoration, Richard III, Home for the Holidays, Two Girls and a Guy, his role as Special Agent John Royce in U.S. Marshals, and Black and White.

Personal Life

Robert Downey Jr.'s personal life has been as complex and fascinating as his on-screen characters, marked by periods of intense struggle, profound transformation, and lasting redemption. Born on April 4, 1965, in Manhattan, New York City, he was raised in a creative but chaotic environment. His father, Robert Downey Sr., was an influential filmmaker known for experimental cinema, while his mother, Elsie Downey, was an actress who appeared in several of her husband’s films. From an early age, Downey was immersed in the bohemian culture of 1970s New York, frequently attending film sets and parties. Unfortunately, this early exposure included drugs—his father allowed him to try marijuana at just eight years old, a decision that Downey later described as a tragic and formative moment. This early introduction to substance use would haunt him for decades, setting the stage for a long battle with addiction. As a teenager, Downey moved with his father to California after his parents divorced. He attended Santa Monica High School, where he briefly studied alongside future stars like Rob Lowe and Sean Penn, but dropped out at 17 to pursue acting full-time in New York. He supported himself with jobs ranging from busboy to shoe salesman while studying acting at the famed Perry House. In his early 20s, Downey gained attention for his charisma and versatility, but his personal life was already unraveling behind the scenes. His relationship with actress Sarah Jessica Parker in the 1980s was widely publicized. They were a young Hollywood power couple, but the relationship ultimately ended in 1991 due to Downey's substance abuse issues, which Parker later described as heartbreaking and unmanageable. In 1992, Downey married actress and singer Deborah Falconer after a brief courtship. The couple had a son, Indio Falconer Downey, in 1993. Despite moments of stability, Downey's drug use continued to spiral, leading to multiple arrests, stints in rehab, and jail time throughout the 1990s. His personal and professional life fell apart, and Falconer eventually left him in 2001. During these years, Downey often lived a nomadic lifestyle—homeless at times—and became virtually unemployable in Hollywood, labeled as too high-risk due to his unpredictable behavior. His son Indio would later face his own struggles with addiction, prompting Downey to publicly express deep remorse and a renewed commitment to being a present and supportive father. Downey’s life took a dramatic turn in 2003 while filming *Gothika*, where he met film producer Susan Levin, an executive at Joel Silver’s production company. Despite initial skepticism from Susan, who was wary of his troubled past, their relationship grew strong. Downey proposed to her on the night before her 30th birthday, and the two were married in 2005. Susan, now Susan Downey, became an instrumental figure in his recovery and rebirth, both personally and professionally. She introduced structure, stability, and discipline into his life, encouraging him to fully commit to sobriety. Downey credits Susan as the cornerstone of his transformation, often stating in interviews that she helped save his life. Together, they have two children: a son, Exton Elias Downey, born in 2012, and a daughter, Avri Roel Downey, born in 2014. In his post-recovery life, Downey has embraced a philosophy of wellness, spirituality, and balance. He practices martial arts, particularly Wing Chun, which he says helped him focus and stay sober. He also meditates, maintains a healthy diet, and speaks openly about his personal growth. Though intensely private about his home life, Downey has shared glimpses into the joy and fulfillment he finds in being a father, husband, and family man. His home life is now far removed from the instability of his early years. Downey and Susan co-founded their production company, Team Downey, in 2010, aiming to create meaningful, character-driven stories. The couple often appears at public events together and are seen as one of Hollywood’s most solid and respected partnerships. As of today, Downey continues to inspire not just through his performances but through his personal journey—a story of resilience, love, redemption, and the enduring belief that people can change, no matter how far they’ve fallen.

Addiction

From 1996 to 2001, Robert Downey Jr. faced numerous arrests for drug-related offenses involving substances like cocaine, heroin, and marijuana. Despite undergoing various rehabilitation programs and serving time in both jail and prison, he continued to struggle with addiction. In a 1999 court appearance, Downey famously told a judge that living with addiction felt like having a shotgun in his mouth with his finger on the trigger—and liking the taste of the gun metal. He revealed that his drug use began as early as age eight, introduced by his father. In early 1996, concerned friends Sean Penn and Dennis Quaid intervened, taking Downey to a rehab center in Tucson, but he checked himself out shortly after. In June of that year, he was arrested for possession of drugs and a handgun while speeding on Sunset Boulevard. A month later, he wandered into a neighbor’s home while under the influence and fell asleep in a bed. Though no charges were filed, the incident became infamously known as the “Goldilocks incident.” Following this, Downey was sentenced to rehab and probation, including mandatory drug testing. In 1997, missing one of those tests led to a six-month jail sentence, followed by further rehabilitation. In 1999, Downey relapsed after filming *Wonder Boys* and was again arrested for missing a drug test. Despite his legal team’s efforts, he received a three-year sentence at a state prison in California. This also cost him professional opportunities—he was fired from the NBC animated series *God, the Devil and Bob* for failing to show up to work. After nearly a year in prison, Downey was granted early release, having served sufficient time across multiple incarcerations. Within a week, he joined the cast of the popular TV series *Ally McBeal*, earning critical acclaim and winning a Golden Globe Award. He even contributed musically to the show. However, despite his success, Downey admitted he was still deeply addicted during this period and indifferent toward his acting career. In November 2000, Downey was arrested again after police found him under the influence at a hotel in Palm Springs. Though he faced a possible prison sentence, he continued working on *Ally McBeal*. In April 2001, he was found barefoot and disoriented in Culver City and tested positive for cocaine. That final arrest led to his dismissal from the show and cost him roles in upcoming projects, including a stage production of *Hamlet* directed by Mel Gibson. In July 2001, he pleaded no contest to charges from the Palm Springs arrest and avoided jail through court-ordered rehab and probation under California’s Proposition 36. By this time, Downey was nearly bankrupt, considered uninsurable in Hollywood, and unemployed. Director Woody Allen later revealed that he had wanted to cast Downey and Winona Ryder in *Melinda and Melinda* but was unable to do so because insurance companies refused to cover them due to their legal histories. In 2000, *People* magazine published a report suggesting Downey had been diagnosed with bipolar disorder. His stepmother said it explained his ongoing struggles with sobriety. A psychiatrist claimed Downey had even written her from prison asking for help. However, in a 2007 *Esquire* interview, Downey denied ever being diagnosed with bipolar disorder, stating that past assessments were unreliable because he had been using drugs at the time. He said a proper diagnosis could only happen once someone was truly sober.

Recovery

After five years of struggling with substance abuse, arrests, rehabilitation, and relapse, Robert Downey Jr. decided to commit to full recovery and make a return to his acting career. In a 2004 interview with Oprah Winfrey, he reflected on his failed attempts to control his addiction, saying that when someone wonders if they should go to rehab, it's usually already clear they need help. He acknowledged that after his final arrest in April 2001, he realized he couldn't keep repeating the same cycle. He said he finally reached out for help with real commitment, noting that while overcoming addiction isn't as hard as it seems, the hardest part is making the decision to change. Downey’s first job after rehab came in August 2001 when he appeared in the music video for Elton John's song “I Want Love.” The video director, Sam Taylor-Wood, used the final take because Downey appeared relaxed and understated, which Elton John praised. In 2003, Mel Gibson—his friend and *Air America* co-star—paid Downey’s insurance bond so he could star in *The Singing Detective*, marking the start of his comeback. That opportunity opened doors for Downey, though producers often required strict clauses in his contracts, such as withholding a portion of his salary until filming was complete, as was the case with the 2003 film *Gothika*. Downey began landing more roles in both independent and mainstream films. He starred in *Kiss Kiss Bang Bang*, a noir comedy directed by Shane Black, which further reestablished his career. Other notable performances included roles in *A Guide to Recognizing Your Saints*, *Good Night, and Good Luck*, *A Scanner Darkly* (where he played a drug addict), and *Fur*, a fictionalized biopic of photographer Diane Arbus. In 2004, Downey released a music album titled *The Futurist*, which featured original songs and artwork he created with his son Indio. The album received mixed reviews, and Downey later stated he likely wouldn’t release another, feeling that the effort wasn’t worth the reward. He also ventured back into television, voicing a character named Patrick Pewterschmidt on *Family Guy*. The opportunity came after Downey personally reached out to the show's producers, as his son was a fan of the series. Downey also signed a deal with HarperCollins in 2006 to write a memoir described as a candid account of his personal and professional struggles. However, in 2008, he returned the advance and quietly canceled the project without explanation. In 2007, Downey earned acclaim for his role as journalist Paul Avery in David Fincher’s crime thriller *Zodiac*, based on the real-life investigation of the Zodiac Killer. This performance marked another strong step in Downey’s return to critically respected work and mainstream success.

Milestones

Despite the critical acclaim Robert Downey Jr. had earned throughout his career, he had not yet starred in a true blockbuster until 2008, when he led two major hits: *Iron Man* and *Tropic Thunder*. In *Time* magazine’s 2008 list of the 100 most influential people, Ben Stiller praised Downey, noting that he had always been a masterful actor and now finally had the commercial success to match. In 2007, Downey was cast as Tony Stark in *Iron Man*. Director Jon Favreau believed Downey deeply understood the character, drawing parallels between Stark's story and his own. Favreau compared Downey’s potential impact on the franchise to Johnny Depp’s role in *Pirates of the Caribbean*. To prepare for the role, Downey gained over 20 pounds of muscle to embody the armored superhero. When *Iron Man* was released in spring 2008, it grossed over \$585 million worldwide and was met with rave reviews, with many praising Downey’s performance. He quickly signed on for sequels and *The Avengers*, expanding the character's presence in the Marvel Cinematic Universe. His cameo as Tony Stark in *The Incredible Hulk* helped establish continuity between the films. Later that year, Downey appeared in *Tropic Thunder* alongside Ben Stiller and Jack Black. He portrayed Kirk Lazarus, an eccentric, Oscar-winning Australian method actor who undergoes a controversial pigmentation procedure to play an African-American character. While Downey and Stiller were cautious about the potential backlash, they emphasized that the satire targeted self-absorbed actors, not racial stereotypes. The film was well received, grossing \$180 million and staying at the top of the U.S. box office for three consecutive weekends. Downey’s performance earned him an Academy Award nomination for Best Supporting Actor. In 2009, Downey starred in *The Soloist*, a drama based on a true story, which was delayed from its original release date. Though it didn't earn him another Oscar nomination, critics had initially speculated it could. The next major role Downey took on was *Sherlock Holmes*, directed by Guy Ritchie and released in December 2009. The film set records for a Christmas Day release and became one of the highest-grossing films of the year. Downey won a Golden Globe for Best Actor for his performance, humorously thanking his wife Susan for wrongly predicting he wouldn’t win. In 2010, he reprised his role as Tony Stark in *Iron Man 2*, which grossed over \$623 million globally. He also starred in the road-trip comedy *Due Date* with Zach Galifianakis, which grossed over \$211 million. In 2011, he returned as Holmes in *Sherlock Holmes: A Game of Shadows*. In 2012, Downey played Stark once again in *The Avengers*, which became one of the highest-grossing films of all time. His role continued in *Iron Man 3* (2013), *Avengers: Age of Ultron* (2015), *Captain America: Civil War* (2016), *Spider-Man: Homecoming* (2017), *Avengers: Infinity War* (2018), and *Avengers: Endgame* (2019). His portrayal of Tony Stark became central to the Marvel Cinematic Universe’s success. In addition to acting, Downey co-produced *The Judge* (2014) through his company Team Downey, which premiered at the Toronto International Film Festival. In 2019, he also hosted the YouTube documentary series *The Age of A.I.*, further diversifying his career in media.
